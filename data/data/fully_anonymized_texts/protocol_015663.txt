Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)] is a modified form of LDL.  It is composed of an LDL particle 
covalently bound to apolipoprotein(a) [apo(a)].  Lip oprotein (a) is distinctly different from 
other plasma lipoproteins given that it is an assembly of two critical elements; a central core linked by a disulfide bridge to a signature [CONTACT_109414], apo(a).  The first element shares similarities with LDL, is rich in cholesterol and contains ApoB .  The other element, 
apo(a), consists of a number of pleated structures, kringles IV and V, of which kringle IV 
type 2 is repeated in a variable number, from 2 to more than 40 times.  The variation in 
kringle IV type 2 repeats gives rise to considerable differences in the size and molecular 
weight of apo(a) and is the major determinant of Lp(a) plasma level (up to 90% ) 
(Dube et al, 2012).

subsequently scavenged by [CONTACT_179968] ( Koschinsky and Marcovina, 2004).

2.3 Rationale 
Beyond the atherogenic potential of the Lp(a) LDL-like core, Lp(a) appears to be the preferential car rier of OxPL  in the plasma and thus closely involved in atherosclerotic 
inflammation (Leibundgut et al, 2013).  The inflammatory response to the accumulation of oxidized lipi[INVESTIGATOR_236460] a pi[INVESTIGATOR_236461], and plaque inflammation is associated with plaque rupture and 
atherothrombotic events ( Tsimikas and Hall, 2012).  It remains to be shown whether 
lowering Lp(a) can reverse these changes, providing a crucial link between lipid lowering 
and decreased inflammatory activity .  Whereas, Lp(a) is relatively refractory to both 
lifestyle and drug intervention, evolocumab 420 mg once monthly  (QM ) reduced Lp(a) by 
[CONTACT_3450] 28% compared to placebo measured using the mean of weeks 10 and 12, 
in phase 3 evolocumab studies (20110114, 20110115, and 20110117).  The safety and 
efficacy of evolocumab 420 mg subcutaneous  (SC) QM in adults has been extensively 
studied in phase [ADDRESS_286180] decade FDG -PET/CT has shown great potential in visualizing, quantifying, 
and characterizing atherosclerotic inflammation non-invasively ( Rosenbaum et al, 2012) 
and is a highly sensitive modality that can be utilized as a surrogate imaging marker for vessel wall inflammation ( Joshi et al , 2011).   FDG -PET/CT imaging has been shown to 
be reproducible for imaging of atherosclerotic plaque inflammation ( Rudd et al 2007) and 
the FDG uptake signal correlates with macrophage content ( Tawakol et al , 2006).  In
