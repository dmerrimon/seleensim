This study is being conducted by the Pediatric Eye Disease Investigator Group (PEDIG) and is 139 
funded through a cooperative agreement from the National Eye Institute.  It is one of a series of 140 
randomized trials and observational studies conducted by PEDIG  that address management of 141 
intermittent exotropia (IXT)  in children.  142 
 143 
1.1 Intermittent Exotropia  144 
The most common type of childhood -onset exotropia is IXT,1-4 with a reported incidence of 145 
32.1/100,00 0,1 occurring more frequently in females5 and in Asian children.4, 6, 7 IXT is a term 146 
used to describe several  subgroup s of intermittent exodeviations, namely basic, true divergence 147 
excess, pseudo divergence excess , and convergence insuff iciency  IXT. Many cases  of 148 
exodeviations are characterized by an exotropia that occurs primarily for distance viewing, but 149 
may also be present at near. Normal binocular single vision and normal stereoacuity are  usually  150 
present at near when the exotropia is  controlled , although stereoacuity can be reduced  in about 151 
25% of cases .8, 9 152 
 153 
The current management of IXT includes  observation as well as surgical and non -surgical 154 
interventions .8 Many practitioners treat IXT patients using non -surgical approaches such as part - 155 
time patching, over -minus lenses, vision therapy10, 11 or prism.12, 13  156 
 157 
1.2 Prism Treatment for IXT 158 
One non-surgical treatment  for IXT is base-in corrective or relieving prism ,14, 15 which eliminates 159 
or reduces the convergence demand , respectively . Corrective prism is a term used when  the full 160 
angle of deviation is corrected with prism while relieving prism provides prism cor rection less 161 
than the full angle of deviation.  Although corrective base-in prism for IXT has been reported to 162 
have a favorable treatment benefit in two small  retrospective studies,12, 13 its clinical 163 
effectiveness (either using corrective or relieving prism) has not yet been studied prospectively 164 
in either randomized or non -randomized controlled trials . 165 
 166 
1.3 Previous Studies of Prism Therapy for IXT  167 
While many studies have reported use of prism as part of a multi -faceted non -surgical treatment 168 
approach  and adjunct to surgical treatmen t of strabismus in general , we are aware of only two 169 
small studies that have evaluated corrective prism alone as an intervention specifically for IXT:   170

1.4 Possible Mechanisms of Prism Therapy  218 
Whether relieving or correcting prism is used, t he mechanism for improvement of IXT with 219 
prism therapy is largely unknown. In t he short term, prism may improve the ability of the child 220 
to control the exodeviation by reducing  or eliminating  the fusional convergence demand 221 
necessary to align the eyes (less convergence is required for bifoveal stimulation with the prism 222 
than without) . In the longer term, prism therapy for IXT may benefit the individual by increasing 223 
the time of fusion and perhaps weakening suppression, leading to an improvement in the ability 224 
to control the exodeviation.12 The majority of patient s with IXT typically have normal sensory 225 
fusion and reduced compensating fusional con vergence ability ,14-16 and therefore  the adage that 226 
“sensory fusion is the best orthoptics” forms the basis for this treatment; the anticipation is that 227 
better sensory fusion will encourage the development of better motor control .17  228

29May2019     1-3 1.5 Methods of Prescribing Prism for IXT  230 
There is currently no universally accepted formula or method for determining the optimal prism 231 
treatment for patients with IXT.  The amount of prism prescribed to treat IXT in previous studies 232 
and in clinical pra ctice varies and is based on 3 philosophical approaches:  233 
1.  Corrective prism is prescribed for the full angle of deviatio n. 234 
2. Customized prism: the minimum prism required to comfortably improve fusion (e.g., 235 
better control, change of diplopia or suppression r esponse to a fusion response on testing, 236 
decrease in the frequency of the IXT based on cover -uncover testing ).  This approach 237 
could result in corrective or relieving prism being prescribed, albeit for IXT, the 238 
customized approach usually results in relievi ng prism and rarely in corrective prism.  239 
3. A fixed amount of relieving prism (e.g., always prescribe 5∆ or 10∆) or a prism 240 
magnitude that is a percentage of the maximum exodeviation : The percentage of the 241 
deviation to correct is typically 33% to 50% of the maximum deviation18 and is usually 242 
divided equally between the two eyes.14, 18  243 
Regardless of which method is used to determine the amount, prism can be prescribed by adding 244 
Fresnel Press -On prism to spectacles  or grinding prism into the lenses, Although Fresnel prism is 245 
a treatment option, most patients prefer ground -in prism because of  better cosmetic appearance 246 
and improved clarity of vision.  247 
 248 
1.6 Potential Problems with  Prescribing Corrective Prism  249 
Corrective prism is typically prescribed by dividing the total prism deviation and placing half as 250 
base-in prism in front of each eye to enco urage bifoveal fusion.  Despite these attempts to induce 251 
bifoveal stimulation, one can argue that this situation may not be ideal for patients who continue 252 
to suppress when fully aligned .  In such cases, it is possible that the prisms either w ould have no 253 
benefit to the long term improvement of control of the IXT or prism adaptation could  occur.18 254 
Additionally, the use of corrective prism is less practical in larger deviations as more optical 255 
aberrations may occur with larger prism magnitudes, in addition to the spectacle lenses being 256 
larger and heavier.  257 
 258 
1.7 Potential Problems with  Prescribing Customized Prism  259 
Another approach to prescribing prism for treatment of IXT is a customized approach, where the 260 
amount of base -in prism to prescribe is the minimum amount of prism that is required to 261 
comfortably improve some aspect of sensorimotor fusion (e.g., better cont rol, change of diplopia 262 
or suppression response to a fusion response on testing, decrease the frequency of the IXT based 263 
on cover -uncover testing, increase in stereoacuity) as determined by testing in the office.  264 
Because of the variable nature of IXT, the  amount of prism magnitude needed for an individual 265 
patient might vary from one moment to another. In addition, this approach is quite dependent on 266 
the subjective interpretation of the examiner. Finally, prism adaptation could occur for relieving 267 
prism as well, thus increasing the magnitude of the deviation.  For these reasons, a customized 268 
method of prescribing prism for IXT is not conducive to a standardized study protocol.  269 
 270 
1.8 Prism Adaptation  271 
Prism adaptation is a term that describes sensorimotor adaptation to prism treatment and can be 272 
part of the normal vergence system.19  However, when pri sms are placed to correct the angle of 273 
deviation in some patients, especially with acquired esotropia, the patient “adapts” to the prism 274 
in place, thus effectively making the deviation larger.  The Prism Adaptation Study demonstrated 275 
greater surgical succe ss in patients with acquired esotropia when the larger, prism -adapted, angle 276 
was targeted for surgery.20 277

1.9 Determining the Dose of Prism for the Current Study  288 
The consensus of the Planning Committee was to prescribe relieving prism.  This decision was 289 
based primarily on the clinical experience of the Planning Committee.  Consensus was reached 290 
to prescribe a base -in prism magnitude that was 40% of the largest exodeviation at distance or 291 
near for a maximum  eligible exodeviation of 35∆ at both distance and near. This amount of 292 
relieving prism was determined based on Caloroso’s residual vergence demand guidelines,14 293 
prism guidelines from Roper -Hall,18 and expert opinion. Given the maximum deviation of 35∆, 294 
the largest magnitude of prism to be prescribed will be 14∆ (7∆ for each eye). This magnitude of 295 
ground -in prism was agreed to be feasible in terms of appearance , weight, and optical 296 
aberrations.  The minimum prism dose would be 6∆ (3∆ for each eye) based on the study -defined 297 
exodeviation of  at least  16∆ at distance.  298

1.10 Public Health Importance of Proposed Randomized Clinical Trial  300 
Although prism treatment for IXT is used  in clinical practice by some eye care providers , there 301 
have been no randomized clinical trials evaluating its effectiveness.  If prism treatment can 302 
successfully restore binocular alignment and facilitate improved  control of the IXT without the 303 
need for surgery or office visits for other treatments , there potentially would be a significant cost 304 
savings.  On the ot her hand, evidence that prism treatment  for IXT is not effective would  reduce 305 
the utilization of this treatment , allowing children to receive more effective treatments with less 306 
delay and avoiding the cost of unnecessary glasses .  307

1.11 Questions Related to Pri sm Therapy  309 
There have been no rigorous studies that address the following important questions related to 310 
prism therapy:  311 
 312 
1. Does prism therapy have an initial short -term therapeutic effect on IXT while wearing 313 
prism (over a number of weeks)?  314 
2. Does prism therap y continue to have a long -term therapeutic benefit for IXT while 315 
wearing prism spectacles (over many months or years)?  316 
3. Does prism therapy have a long -term therapeutic effect for IXT when prism spectacles 317 
are tapered and discontinued ? 318 
 319 
In the initial planni ng stages of the study we considered a full -scale RCT to address th ese 320 
questions, likely comparing prism therapy to non -prism spectacles. On further discussion, it 321 
became apparent that the lack of published data using relieving prism therapy lends itself t o the 322 
need for a pilot study to answer question 1 prior to moving forward with questions 2 -3.  As a first 323 
step, we determined that a pilot study was needed to evaluate whether there is a short -term 324 
therapeutic treatment effect while wearing base -in relievi ng prism over a period of weeks, noting 325

1.12 Definitions of Treatment Response  329 
Previous PEDIG studies have used a “control score” (the proportion of time  that the deviation is 330 
manifest) to judge improvement or worsening of IXT or response to treatment.21-24 A single 331 
control score has been found to be highly variable.25 26 Due to this variability, a  more adequate 332 
representation of control during the day can better be achieved by  measuring  the “triple control 333 
score,”26 which is a mea n of 3 control measures obtained at various times during an examination.   334 
 335 
Beca use the purpose of the treatment of IXT with prism  spectacles is to better align the eyes for a 336 
greater proportion of time, and single binocular vision with high grade stereoacuity is associated 337 
with good ocular alignment, it is reasonable to primarily focus on “control” of the distance 338 
deviation as the first step in evaluating the effectiveness of prism  treatment  as in prior overminus 339 
studies (IXT3, IXT5) .23   340 
 341 
1.13 Timing of Intervention, Outcome for Current S tudy  342 
To eva luate the initial response to prism  spectacles , this 8 -week pilot randomized clinical trial 343 
(RCT)  is proposed.  If  a reasonable initial response without significant adverse effect s is found , a 344 
subsequent full -scale RCT would evaluate the long -term effectiv eness of prism  (e.g., over 1 345 
year) and then evaluate the subsequent effectiveness of maintaining control after the prism  346 
treatment has been discontinued ( e.g., 6 months after discontinuation of prism ). This approach 347 
was successfully used  previously to explore overminus spectacles as a treatment for IXT through 348 
an 8-week pilot RCT (IXT3) , followed by a full -scale longer RCT to evaluate the long -term 349 
effectiveness on treatment and after discontinuation of treatment (IXT5).   350

1.14 Study Objective  352

prism to spectacles with out prism  is to determine whether to proceed to a full -scale, longer -term 354 
randomized trial . This decision will be based primarily on asses sing the initial (8-week) 355 
response to prism  by comparing  treatment groups on  the following outcomes : 356

 The proportion of participants demonstrating a “ treatment response, ” defined as ≥1 point 358 
improvement in  the mean distance IXT control  score  without spontaneous exotropia during 359 
control testing (secondary  outcome ) 360 
 The proportion of partic ipants reporting a dverse effects  and good/excellent spectacle wear 361 
compliance  362
